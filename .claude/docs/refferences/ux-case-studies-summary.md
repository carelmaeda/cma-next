# UX Case Studies — Summary
*Source: [IxDF — What are UX Case Studies?](https://ixdf.org/literature/topics/ux-case-studies)*

---

## What is a UX Case Study?

A UX case study is a detailed narrative and analysis of a UX design project. It illustrates a designer's process and solution to a specific challenge — covering the problem, research, design decisions, and impact. Designers include case studies in their portfolios to demonstrate skills and value to employers and clients.

---

## Why UX Case Studies Matter

- Provide **concrete evidence** of a designer's ability to tackle complex challenges.
- Build **trust and credibility** through transparency about approach, thinking, and methods.
- Foster **personal and professional growth** through reflection and self-analysis.
- Strengthen **communication skills** — articulating reasoning and presenting data effectively.
- Give recruiters insight into a designer's thought process beyond resumes and qualifications.

---

## How to Approach UX Case Studies

- Recruiters typically decide within **5 minutes** whether a candidate is a good fit.
- Prioritize **quality over quantity** when selecting case studies for a portfolio.
- Case studies should accurately and positively represent the designer.
- Must include: clear process documentation, compelling visuals, and error-free writing.

---

## How to Build Successful UX Case Studies

- Write with an **active voice** and story structure: beginning, middle, and end (never a flat report).
- Always **get permission** from employers/clients before featuring their work.
- **Anonymize confidential data** (e.g., change figures to percentages, remove unnecessary details).
- Be mindful of any **NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)** obligations.

---

## Step-by-Step Guide to Creating a UX Case Study

1. **Choose the right project** — Select work that best showcases relevant skills; ensure you have permission to share it.
2. **Define the problem** — Clearly articulate the challenge, its significance, and background context.
3. **Establish your role** — Detail your responsibilities and highlight teamwork and collaboration.
4. **Describe the process** — Include research methods (interviews, surveys), user personas, journey maps, and real insights.
5. **Illustrate the design journey** — Show wireframes, prototypes, iterations, and explain design decisions with supporting visuals.
6. **Highlight testing & iteration** — Detail usability tests, key findings, and how feedback drove improvements.
7. **Showcase the final solution** — Present high-fidelity mockups and highlight key features and functionalities.
8. **Conclude with results** — Share metrics/data showing impact (e.g., improved usability, higher engagement) and lessons learned.
9. **Present the story** — Ensure a coherent narrative with integrated visuals; keep it concise and jargon-free.
10. **Final review** — Proofread thoroughly and seek feedback from peers or mentors.

## Storytelling Framework

### Aristotle's Elements
| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Plot** | The career aspect to highlight (consistent with the target role) |
| **Character** | Designer's expertise and teamwork |
| **Theme** | Goals, motivations, and obstacles of the project |
| **Diction** | Friendly, professional, jargon-free tone |
| **Melody** | Passion for design as a lifelong interest |
| **Décor** | Balance of text and visuals |
| **Spectacle** | The "wow factor" or unexpected discovery |

### Freytag's 5-Part Pyramid Structure
1. **Exposition (Hook)** — Problem statement, solution outline, designer's role (4–5 sentences).
2. **Rising Action** — Obstacles, constraints, and the design process leading to key moments.
3. **Climax** — The story's most intriguing or pivotal point.
4. **Falling Action** — How insights and iterations shaped the final product.
5. **Resolution** — Final results, business impact, and lessons learned (4–5 sentences).

---

## Key Principles for a Strong Case Study

- Tell a design story that **progresses meaningfully**.
- Reinforce each *"what"* with a *"how"* and *"why"*.
- **Balance "I" with "we"** to acknowledge team contributions.
- Use visuals (screenshots, wireframes, personas, flowcharts, journey maps) to support the narrative.
- Make the case study **scannable** with headings and subheadings.
- Remove anything that doesn't advance the story or explain the thought process.
