# How to Make Your Own Awesome UX Design Portfolio
> **Source:** [CareerFoundry](https://careerfoundry.com/en/blog/ux-design/ux-design-portfolio-tips/)
> **Author:** Tom Cotterill | **Updated:** December 6, 2022

---

## Overview

A UX design portfolio is essential for landing roles at any career level. It acts as a first impression before a hiring manager meets you in person, and it helps differentiate you in an increasingly competitive market where CVs alone are no longer enough.

---

## 1. Is a UX Portfolio Necessary?

Yes — arguably more important than a CV. Recruiters spend only about 6 seconds scanning a CV. A portfolio lets you show personality, process, and cultural fit in ways a CV cannot. It's also vital for hybrid roles (e.g., combining UX, UI, and strategic work).

---

## 2. Purpose of a UX Portfolio

- Define who you are, what you do, and your UX specialization
- Show cultural suitability for the company you're applying to
- Stand out from the crowd with personality and style
- Demonstrate value beyond what a CV can convey

---

## 3. What to Include in a UX Portfolio

The single most important thing: **show your process**, not just polished final designs.

For each project, include:
- The problem you were solving
- Who you worked with
- Tools used
- Discovery/research phases
- The design process (wireframes, prototypes, personas, user journeys, etc.)
- The final outcome and what happened after handoff
- Storytelling between steps — explain *why* you moved from one stage to the next

---

## 4. Tips by Experience Level

### Beginners & Juniors
- Inject personality, even with few projects
- Include personal projects and course work
- Show failures honestly — grit and humility are valued
- Hiring managers want cultural fit, not perfection; skills can be learned on the job

### Seniors & Managers
- Showcase team work and strategic leadership rather than old hands-on projects
- Show how you set strategy, delegated tasks, and delivered outcomes
- Include data (ROI, improved UX metrics) to back up results
- If you mentored juniors, show before/after examples of their work

---

## 5. Presentation Tips

- Keep a consistent visual style (color palette, fonts) throughout
- Annotate your work, but avoid being too text-heavy
- Visual appeal signals attention to detail — even non-UI designers should present well
- Consider using **video** to show user testing sessions, product demos, or a short personal intro

---

## 6. UX Researchers

Researchers should also have portfolios — but focused on **impact**, not UI screens.

Include:
- Client/project context
- The problem you were brought in to solve
- Your research approach
- Results (clearly formatted)
- What your findings meant for the rest of the project

---

## 7. How Many Projects to Include

- **Maximum 3 projects** — quality over quantity
- Juniors: 1–2 projects is fine
- Choose projects where you had the most impact
- If targeting a specific sector, include at least one relevant project (even if it's a personal one)

---

## 8. Testing Your Portfolio

Aim for a hiring manager to understand your work in **30–60 seconds**.

Best practices:
- Conduct simple user tests with friends or family
- Use clear navigation, sub-headings, and a defined process
- Remove anything that doesn't represent your best work
- Include a clear project overview so readers don't have to dig for basic info
- Test with different types of reviewers (team leads, recruiters, PMs) and give them different goals

---

## 9. Handling NDAs

- Always ask the client first
- If needed, anonymize project details while keeping the problem, process, and outcomes
- Juniors: try to take roles where you can share your work to build your portfolio for future opportunities

---

## 10. Building a Portfolio with No Experience

Options to get started:
1. **Take a course** — apply UX principles through curriculum projects
2. **Volunteer** for a charity or non-profit — real users, real problems
3. **Join hackathons** — build portfolio pieces quickly while networking

---

## 11. Where to Host Your Portfolio

**Recommended: Personal website** (e.g., yourname.com)
- Easier to share and remember
- Can include About page, blog, video, personality
- Good for personal branding and SEO
- Consider a **supplementary PDF** for targeting specific sectors/companies

**Other platforms:** Behance, Dribbble (useful but not critical)

---

## 12. Finding Inspiration

- Browse top UX portfolio examples and breakdowns online
- Look at graduate portfolios for realistic benchmarks
- Follow communities like UX Collective for curated examples

---

## 13. Key Takeaways

- A UX portfolio is non-negotiable at every career stage
- **Process > polish** — show how you think, not just what you made
- Tailor content to seniority level and target audience
- Personality and cultural fit matter, especially for junior roles
- Test your portfolio like you'd test a product
